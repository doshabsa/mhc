Web 1.0


Web 1.0 featured the dawn of the internet. As a wild west, many of the web features that we have grown accustomed to today did not exist. Design often took more of secondary seat to information sharing, which gave the entire 1990–2005-time span of Web 1.0 a unique look that is considered nostalgic (by some.)

Large portions of the web space at this time were focused on companies and their home pages. It was a means of pushing their brands and collecting page views. Many sites were merely directories or sources of where to find the information you were looking for. It was in this time that many advertisements (much like today) featured flashing banners or pop-up windows. Unfortunately, there came a point in which many viruses were shared through clicking on this kind of content.

Also, during this time, the predominant (only) language in which to write web pages was HTML. Due to the limited capabilities of the language, popular web sites from this time have a very distinct look. Colours were mostly limited and creative applications such as Adobe Photoshop were only beginning to be developed.


Common Themes


  • Read-Only/Text Content
  • Static/Non-changing Elements
  • Invasive Advertisements
  • Reload Response/Refresh
  • Copyrighting

Web 2.0


In the early 2000s, Web 2.0 was only getting started. Its course has steered it toward a greater focus on the community. It ushered in a plethora of new types of personal material, ranging from blog postings to less intrusive/interactive advertising.

By making many objects on the web page interactive, much of the new content that was becoming available was more personalised for the user and allowed them to feel more included.

Established web outlets made a much stronger push to disseminate their material over the Internet while still maintaining their viewership.

At this time, the first of many web programmes targeted at promoting interaction and securing regular users for websites became accessible. Many community sites (offshoots of MySpace) popped up around this period, but they all eventually succumbed to Facebook as the years passed. Many more accessibility elements and criteria become the norm while building web sites as a result of the increased focus on community. The ability to respond to new phones began to emerge as well, especially when screen phones became more popular than computers and proven to be significantly more accessible to many people.


Common Themes


  • Tagging/Classification
  • Page Responsiveness
  • Online Communities
  • User Accounts/Logins
  • More CSS/JSON

Web 3.0


Based on blockchain technology, Web 3.0 is a vision for the Internet's future. This iteration is expected to incorporate decentralisation (the shift away from large groupings), with smaller and more varied groups having access to the same resources and access as the majority.

Non-fungible tokens (NFTs) have emerged as a result, which are used in conjunction with bitcoin as a payment method.

As a result, a variety of alternative cryptocurrencies have evolved, many of which focus on the ability to exchange money without the involvement of a bank or government. There have been various discussions regarding how Web 3.0 can increase data security and privacy beyond the capabilities of Web 2.0 platforms.

Several platforms have expressed an interest in incorporating web 3.0 technologies into their websites. As a result, many of the tokens and NFCs that are currently accessible may very likely become the future money as Web 3.0 evolves.


Common Themes


  • Movememnt away from large groups/firms
  • Security, scalability, privacy
  • File archives
  • Increased microtransactions
  • New file systems
The internet is currently evolving and developing into its most recent form. Following some research into current trends, it appears that the industries of bitcoin and NFCs are experiencing significant growth. While it's great to see more security and safety being taken into account in future advancements, I can't help but be concerned about the rise of microtransactions. There appears to be an opportunity for small organisations to seize a future in which we may fully decentralise, but there also seems to be an opportunity for huge tech companies to invest in and absorb the rising industry, in my opinion. I'm particularly interested in the greater ownership of personal data, which is one of Web 3.0's many advances. As encryption and verification technologies advance, I hope to see a day where we can all sell our data rather than having it taken from us. With the advancement of blockchain algorithms, there is a great deal of potential for both positive and harmful outcomes.

The rise of AI learning is also intriguing. As technology advances, AI will be able to better forecast not only what we want, but also what will be advantageous to us. It might be as simple as completing our sentences or as complex as machine learning that can predict purchasing trends based on sales data. A machine's ability to absorb and interpret data is significantly superior to what we can achieve by hand.

Our data, on the other hand, may be assigned and linked to anything we do. The social credit score, which was adopted in some Chinese regions and is used to determine access to services and maybe resources depending on your personal score is an example of this.

The self-driving networks are also very intriguing. Cars in big fleets would be able to control themselves thanks to the encryption provided by blockchain, as any data they collect would be able to be shared and reported to other vehicles in the region. Any changes in road conditions might be relayed across the network, and passengers may be diverted to escape congestion or emergencies without their knowledge. When situations emerge, the self-driving network would be able to manage, correct, and self-configure as needed. Imagine a day when your car drives itself to get an oil change while you sleep; this is just the tip of the technological iceberg.
